# Game Design & Creative Vision

This document describes the creative vision behind TreasureRun: the world it takes place in, the ideas that shaped it, and the question the game quietly carries beneath its mechanics.

The main README focuses on TreasureRun as an engineering project. This document focuses on the other half: the game as a world.

## The Game, Briefly

TreasureRun is a Minecraft treasure-hunt mini-game. Players explore a stage, collect chests, build a score, and encounter a wandering trader who exchanges items gathered during play.

On the surface, the game is simple and fast. Underneath, it is built around a more deliberate fictional setting: a floating, post-collapse world where trade, language, memory, and survival all matter.

## The Setting

TreasureRun is set on an artificial island floating in the open ocean, far from any visible coastline.

The stage combines two contrasting moods. One side is cold and futuristic: glass, light, artificial structures, and a sense of engineered survival. The other side is warmer and more human: a wandering trader, llamas, exchange, movement, and small acts of continuity.

That contrast is important to the world. The futuristic architecture suggests that humanity has rebuilt something. The trader and llamas suggest that humanity is still fragile, ordinary, and dependent on small forms of trust.

I wanted the stage to feel like science fiction, but not a distant or sterile version of it. Even if the world has collapsed or changed, people still need food, language, shelter, trade, and companionship.

## Why the Ocean

The ocean setting is not only a visual choice.

There is a long tradition of imagining utopia across water. Plato's Atlantis is an island civilization beyond the known world. Thomas More's *Utopia* is also placed on an island, somewhere separated from ordinary political life. Across many works of literature and political imagination, the sea becomes a place where another kind of society might exist.

TreasureRun uses that idea in a more fragile way.

The world of the game imagines a future where land-based life has become unstable, and people have adapted by moving onto the ocean. The floating island is not paradise in a simple sense. It is a survival structure, a place built after loss.

Modern floating-city concepts, including projects such as Dogen City, also influenced how I thought about this setting. I was interested in the point where speculative fiction and real engineering begin to overlap: a future where people may have to build distributed, ocean-based settlements in response to environmental and social pressure.

The ocean in TreasureRun is therefore not just background scenery. It represents distance from the old world, and the possibility of building something different after collapse.

## Barter and the Wandering Trader

In TreasureRun's world, ordinary currency has weakened or lost meaning.

If the systems behind money collapse, people return to older forms of exchange: food, tools, materials, information, and favors. The wandering trader represents that kind of economy. He moves between places, carrying value in practical goods rather than abstract currency.

This was partly inspired by post-collapse fiction such as *Waterworld*, where floating communities and barter economies suggest a world that has lost many of its former systems but has not lost the need for exchange.

I find barter interesting because it is both simple and emotionally heavy. It shows that society still exists, but in a reduced and more immediate form. People are no longer trading through institutions. They are trading through need.

That feeling is part of TreasureRun's atmosphere.

## Multilingualism as Worldbuilding

TreasureRun's multilingual design is an engineering feature, but it is also part of the game's worldbuilding.

A floating, post-collapse society would not naturally have only one language. People would arrive from different places, carrying their languages, memories, and older cultures with them.

That is why multilingual support matters to the fiction as well as to the code. Languages in TreasureRun are not only interface options. They are traces of the people who might have reached this floating world.

The inclusion of languages such as Latin, Classical Chinese, Sanskrit, Old Japanese, and ASL Gloss also reflects a personal interest in languages as memory. Some languages are widely spoken. Others are historical, sacred, scholarly, or symbolic. In a world after collapse, those languages could become a way of carrying cultural memory forward.

In that sense, multilingualism is part of the game's emotional structure: it connects survival with memory.

## Creative Influences

TreasureRun is influenced by several ideas and works:

- Plato's Atlantis, as an image of a lost ocean civilization
- Thomas More's *Utopia*, as an island society separated from ordinary political life
- Post-collapse ocean fiction such as *Waterworld*
- Contemporary floating-city concepts such as Dogen City
- The visual tension between cold futuristic infrastructure and warmer human-scale imagery
- Multilingual environments where language itself becomes part of identity and memory

These influences are not used as direct references or copies. They are part of the background thinking that shaped the stage, the mood, and the direction of the project.

## The Question Behind the Game

The question behind TreasureRun is simple:

If the old systems collapsed, what would people still treasure?

The game does not ask this directly. Players are meant to move, search, collect, trade, and score. But underneath those actions, the world is built around that question.

What remains valuable when currency loses meaning?
What kinds of exchange survive?
What memories do people carry?
What languages do they keep?
What does a new society look like when it is built on water?

I do not think a small game needs to explain all of this to the player. But I do think a game can be shaped by a question, even quietly.

## Development Process

This vision did not come together cleanly.

Before I could continue building, I had to stop and ask myself what kind of game I actually wanted to make, and what I wanted the game to express. I did not want TreasureRun to be only a programming exercise. I wanted it to become a small fantasy world that combined the things I genuinely care about: oceanic utopias, science fiction, ecology, multilingual culture, history, music, philosophy, UFO-like strangeness, and my personal affection for languages such as German.

That process took longer than I expected. The stage, the colors, the music, the trader, the llamas, and the contrast between cold glass architecture and warmer human-scale imagery all took a lot of trial and error. I kept revising because I wanted the game to feel like one coherent world, not just a collection of features.

The engineering work has been difficult in a concrete way: Java, Spigot, Fabric, ResourcePacks, MySQL, CI, i18n, and debugging. The creative work has been difficult in a different way. There is no compiler error that tells you when a world feels wrong. You have to keep revising until the atmosphere begins to feel honest.

TreasureRun is still unfinished, but it has become the first project where the engineering and the creative vision feel connected to me.


## Why This Matters to the Project

TreasureRun is not only a technical portfolio piece. It is also a small attempt to build a world with a coherent idea behind it.

The engineering side shows systems thinking: localization architecture, client/server boundaries, persistence, testing, and deployment.

The creative side shows why those systems exist inside the game: multilingualism, survival, exchange, and the memory of older worlds.

Together, they make TreasureRun more than a collection of features. They make it a project with both technical structure and creative direction.
